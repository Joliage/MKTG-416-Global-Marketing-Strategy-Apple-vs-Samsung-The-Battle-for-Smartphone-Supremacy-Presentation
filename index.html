Chapter 1 · The Worldwide Marketplace

Everyday life is already global

03

The opening idea

Consumers buy from companies all over the world every week — a shirt sewn in Vietnam, a phone designed in California and assembled in China. Globalization has fundamentally changed how marketers approach business.

To succeed abroad, marketers learn new cultures, work with partners who speak other languages, and navigate different laws and buying motives.

Why start here for phones

  • The smartphone is the textbook example of a globally marketed everyday product.
  • Apple and Samsung both compete in 100+ countries with adapted mixes.
  • Components, design, assembly, and sales span dozens of nations — a fully global value chain.

Chapter 1 · Types of Global Businesses · LO #1

MNCs, born-globals, home & host

04
Multinational corporation (MNC): an organization conducting business in at least one country other than the home country where it is headquartered.
Born-global firm: a business that operates in two or more countries from inception.
Home country: the nation where the business is located or that houses its main headquarters.
Host country: the nation being targeted for expansion by a company.

Applied to the case

Apple (home country: United States) and Samsung (home country: South Korea) are both large MNCs. When Apple sells the iPhone in India, India is the host country. Neither is "born-global," but Samsung's parent group has been internationally diversified across industries for decades.

Chapter 1 · Company Orientation

Four ways leaders see the world

05

Ethnocentric

Home-country employees and policies run everything abroad. Strong control and fast competency transfer, but weak local adaptation.

Polycentric

Locals run host-country operations. Cheaper and better-adapted, but coordination across units is harder.

Regiocentric

Managers drawn from a region (e.g., the Pacific Rim). Knowledge transfers within the region; control across a diverse set is the challenge.

Geocentric

Best people and systems regardless of country; a truly global orientation. Adapts fastest, but global search and coordination cost more.

Chapter 1 · The Essence of Marketing

Marketing is an exchange of value

07

Definition

Marketing is discovering consumer needs and wants, creating the products that meet those needs, and then pricing, promoting, and delivering those products and services.

International marketing: using the marketing mix to meet the needs and wants of consumers in foreign markets.
Exchange of value (AMA view): creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, and society at large.

Money is common, but not required

Any trade that satisfies both parties is marketing — goods for goods, or goods for services. The defining feature is mutual value, not currency.

Why it frames the case: Apple and Samsung each win only when buyers feel they receive value worth the price. Apple's value is an integrated premium experience; Samsung's is breadth and choice.

08

  • Product: Apple = one integrated device; Samsung = a wide range plus the larger "phablet" screen.

Chapter 1 · The Promotional Mix

Promotion is itself a bundle

09

Advertising

Paid, non-personal messages. Apple's 1984 spot and "Think Different" are landmarks.

Sales promotion

Short-term incentives. Carrier bundles and trade deals push volume.

Personal selling

Face-to-face. Apple Stores and carrier reps shape the purchase.

Earned media and direct outreach. Samsung's Oscars selfie was a PR coup.

Chapter 1 · Markets, Needs & Wants

From needs to demand

11

Need

A necessity of life for survival and well-being. To communicate and connect.

Want

A need expressed as a desire for a specific object. "I want a smartphone."

Demand

The amount buyers will purchase at various price levels. Fewer can afford $850.

Market

People with wants and needs, money to spend, and willingness to spend it.

Why demand is central

A majority may want a flagship phone, but only some can turn that want into demand. In India an Android phone runs ~$100 while an iPhone 5 runs ~$850 — so demand splits sharply by price.

The wants vary by culture

The same need (status, connection) is expressed through different wants in different markets — which is why the needs ladder on the next slide matters for positioning.

Chapter 1 · Segmentation, Targeting & Positioning

STP and differentiation

13

Segmentation

Grouping consumers by needs, attitudes, and interests. International marketers also segment by nationality and custom.

Targeting

Choosing the specific, identifiable segments the company will actually serve with its mix.

Positioning & differentiation

Creating a perception relative to rivals by emphasizing a unique benefit that separates the product from competitors.

Two positioning strategies

  • Apple — differentiation by being singular: one premium phone everywhere, positioned on design, ecosystem, and "cool."
  • Samsung — multi-segment: many phones at many prices, each tuned to a region; 60,000 research staff track trends in fashion and design.

Two valid paths: Apple differentiates by being singular; Samsung differentiates by covering segments Apple ignores. Build a statement yourself on the next slide.

Chapter 1 · Marketing in an International Context

Same essence, more adjustments

15

What changes across borders

The essence of marketing stays the same, but entering a new country means adjusting the mix plus managerial practices, accounting, HR, shipping, and storage.

Two forces decide how much adaptation is needed:

  • The drivers of globalization — forces that open markets.
  • The factors that create complexity — differences that demand adaptation.

Homogenization vs. difference

Sometimes tastes converge and only small tweaks are needed; sometimes a host market is so different that big changes to the mix are required.

The case in one line: Apple bets tastes have converged (one phone everywhere); Samsung bets they haven't (many phones, adapted by region).

Chapter 1 · Drivers of Globalization · LO #3

Four forces opening markets

16
Globalization: the increased interconnectedness of consumers and businesses globally (Figure 1.4).

Channels of Communication

Cheaper, broader communication and social networks let firms reach and hear consumers anywhere.

Case: the Oscars Galaxy selfie hit 3.5M+ retweets.

Lower Transportation Costs

Containerization and cheaper freight make entering more markets viable.

Case: global supply chains ship hardware worldwide.

Immigration & Emigration

Movement of people widens brand exposure and cross-border demand.

Case: diaspora & travel spread phone preferences.

Governmental Actions

Liberalization and lower barriers stimulate flows of goods, capital, and labor.

Case: carrier subsidies set the $199 US iPhone price.

Chapter 1 → Ch. 10 bridge · Product Strategy

Standardize or adapt?

20

The core trade-off

Standardization keeps one mix everywhere: lower cost, consistent brand, faster rollout. Adaptation tailors the mix to each market: better local fit, higher cost and complexity.

  • Apple → standardization: the iPhone 5 "was the same in every world market."
  • Samsung → adaptation: several Galaxy S4 versions with different processors for different regions.

Why neither always wins

The right point on the scale depends on how similar the host market is to home and how price-sensitive demand is. Standardization scales cheaply; adaptation captures buyers a one-size product would miss.

Chapter 1 · International Marketing in Daily Life

Opportunity hides in everyday products

22

The chapter's argument

Marketing can feel glamorous, but most opportunities come from simple, everyday products — bathing, eating, transportation, payment. Commonplace activities reveal target markets that can be served with culturally adapted products.

The phone as daily infrastructure

  • The smartphone has become an everyday-life product — payment, communication, navigation, media.
  • Apple Pay (launched with the iPhone 6) pushed the phone into the daily act of paying.
  • Everyday ubiquity is exactly why both firms fight so hard for each segment.

Chapter 1 · Sustainability · LO #5

Sustainability as a source of profit

23
Sustainability: meeting the needs of the current generation in a way that leaves future generations able to meet their needs.

The chapter reframes sustainability not as a cost but as a driver of innovation and savings — FedEx cut fuel use ~36% while raising capacity ~20%, then sold sustainability consulting.

The five-stage framework (Table 1.2)

  • 1 · Compliance as opportunity — beat regulations early.
  • 2 · Sustainable value chains — supply, operations, returns.
  • 3 · Sustainable products — leverage "green" demand.
  • 4 · New business models — new ways to deliver value.
  • 5 · Next-practice platforms — change the paradigm.

Chapter 1 · Bottom-of-the-Pyramid

Four billion overlooked consumers

25
Bottom-of-the-pyramid: the ~4 billion people globally living on less than $2 per day.

The traditional view said this group lacks the money to be worth targeting. Prahalad countered: at four billion consumers, small per-sale profits aggregate enormously, and serving them drives efficiency and innovation that flow back to richer markets.

Direct line to the case

  • Developing countries show strong demand for inexpensive phones.
  • Samsung's low-cost Android models reach exactly this segment.
  • Apple long had no low-cost option — the gap the iPhone 5C tried, imperfectly, to fill.

Chapter 1 · Ethical Decision-Making

Relativism vs. justice

26

Cultural relativism

An individual's culture should determine what is ethical; what a culture accepts is the standard for doing business there.

The risk: taken to the extreme, it can tacitly excuse practices most people consider wrong, such as child labor.

The justice approach

Treat all people fairly and consistently, regardless of local law.

  • Distributive justice: fairness of outcomes, rewards, and punishments.
  • Procedural justice: fair, reliable use of rules and protocols.

Calls for equivalent pay, conditions, and conduct everywhere a firm operates.

Why Samsung was the threat

  • Galaxy phones run Android; the Note's larger "phablet" screen was a point of difference that drove sales.
  • Phones across the full price range, including inexpensive Android models for developing markets where Apple had no low-cost option.

Case 1-3 · The Post-Jobs Era

Cook's strategic moves

30

iPhone 5C

Lower-priced, colorful, plastic — aimed at buyers who couldn't afford a top-of-line phone, especially in emerging markets.

Beats, $3B

Brought Dr. Dre and Jimmy Iovine in; signaled a bet on music and "fashion electronics."

In-house agency

After worrying its ads had lost their edge, Apple formed an internal team and ran a creative "shootout."

iPhone 6 / Apple Pay

Bigger screens (answering Samsung's phablet) plus mobile payments and the Apple Watch.

Reading the moves through Chapter 1

Each decision is a marketing-mix lever: the 5C is price and product segmentation; the in-house agency is promotion; larger screens are product adaptation to a competitor's point of difference; Apple Pay extends the product into everyday life.

Discussion Question 1

Was the iPhone 5C the right pricing decision?

34

The case for "yes"

  • It addressed a real gap: for years Apple had no lower-cost phone for buyers unwilling or unable to pay a premium.
  • It extended the line into emerging markets (China, India) where price-sensitive demand was large and growing.
  • It protected brand equity better than discounting the flagship — a separate, colorful product, not a cheapened 5s.
  • It still aimed at value: Cook said the goal was "a great phone and a great experience" at lower cost, not a cheap phone.

The case for "no" / the limits

  • At only ~$100 below the 5s, it wasn't cheap enough to beat ~$100 Android phones in markets like India.
  • It risked cannibalizing the flagship without winning many genuinely new buyers.
  • Rapid low-cost competition from Chinese makers (e.g., Xiaomi) tempered the opportunity.

My assessment: directionally right, incompletely executed. Segmenting price-sensitive buyers was sound; the price point sat in an awkward middle. The model on slide 32 shows it pays off only with enough true budget buyers and a wide enough gap.

Discussion Question 2

How has Samsung's global strategy worked so well?

35

Full-range product

Phones at every price point, from inexpensive Android models to flagship Galaxy devices — covering segments Apple ignored.

Regional adaptation

Multiple Galaxy S4 versions with different processors tuned to different regions.

Research-driven

60,000 research staff; designers from psychology, sociology, engineering tracking fashion and design.

Aggressive promotion

Heavy spend, sponsorships, and points of difference like the larger phablet screen.

Mapping to Chapter 1

  • Segmentation & targeting: serves many segments at once.
  • Mix adapted: product, price, place, promotion all vary by market.
  • Drivers of globalization: rides communication channels and emerging-market growth.

Bottom line

Samsung competes by covering the whole market while adapting locally — the opposite of Apple's one-premium-product consistency. Both are legitimate; Samsung's breadth reaches buyers a single flagship cannot.

The model behind the live demos

Pricing model — the core function (runnable excerpt)

// Demand splits by buyer type and price sensitivity. // This is the exact logic running on slide 32. function evaluate(market, gapPct) { const premium = market.premiumShare; // 0..1 const budget = 1 - premium; const sens = market.priceSensitivity; // 0..1 // Flagship-only: budget buyers mostly walk away. const flagBuyers = premium + budget * (1 - sens) * 0.25; // Add a 5C-style phone priced gapPct below flagship. const capture = sens * (gapPct / 100); const newBudget = budget * capture; // genuinely new const cannibalize = premium * capture * 0.4; // shifted down const flagRev = flagBuyers * 1.0; const bothRev = (flagBuyers - cannibalize) * 1.0 + (newBudget + cannibalize) * (1 - gapPct / 100); return { flagRev, bothRev, newBudget }; }

Chapter 1 · Review & Resources

Strategic, tactical & operational implications

37

Strategic

Begins with a SWOT analysis, then a choice among broad differentiation, low-cost, or best-cost.

  • Apple: broad differentiation.
  • Samsung: best-cost across many segments.

Tactical

Every mix element adjusts as a firm goes international; tactics must rest on deep understanding of each market.

  • Localized advertising and retail relationships.
  • Mistakes are assumed; rapid correction matters.

Operational

Day-to-day execution varies by market — payment, body language, bargaining, shipping standards.

  • Consistency with strategy and tactics is the goal.
